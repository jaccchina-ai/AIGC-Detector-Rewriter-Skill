# Qualitative Authorship Restoration (reference module)

This module is the qualitative-specific extension of Technique 29 (Evidence-Based Authorship Restoration). It applies ONLY to qualitative studies and the qualitative parts of mixed-methods studies (interviews, focus groups, observation, case study, ethnography, phenomenology, IPA, narrative inquiry, discourse analysis, grounded theory, thematic/content analysis, document analysis).

It does not replace the main rules. The iron rules still hold: minimal edit, no regeneration, no back-translation, word count ≥ original, no merged paragraphs, all Gates A–J, and absolutely no fabrication. Detector outcomes are post-revision observations, never guarantees.

## Core principle

In qualitative work, genuine authorial voice is shown through *traceable interpretation*, not through artificial irregularity. A legitimate revision helps the reader see how an interpretation arises from materials and analysis that already exist in the thesis. If a new sentence cannot be traced to the study's own materials, codes/themes, real analytical process, declared method, or accurately-cited literature, it must not be added — mark it for author confirmation instead.

Priority order: (1) authenticity of original materials; (2) traceability of interpretation; (3) compatibility with the declared methodology; (4) academic quality; (5) external detector result as observation only.

## Step 1 — Is the passage actually mechanical?

Only act if the passage shows a real problem: identical theme skeletons (theme → quote → generic conclusion), quotations used as decoration, topic-only theme labels, participants flattened into one uniform voice, generic implications/limitations unconnected to the study, or summary-without-significance. If none is present, do not rewrite just to "look human".

## Step 2 — Permitted support sources

A new interpretive sentence may be added only under T29 (evidence-based reconstruction), never under T19 (relocation only), and only with a completed Source Trace block; in normal qualitative de-templating, prefer relocation or light rephrasing of existing wording before adding any new sentence. If the source cannot be identified, mark "author confirmation needed" instead of writing the sentence. When permitted, it must be grounded in: original quotations/observation records/field notes/documents/case evidence actually collected; existing codes, categories, themes, narrative or discourse patterns already developed; the declared analytical approach; the documented setting, participant characteristics, sampling/access; accurately-represented cited literature; or interpretive boundaries that genuinely follow from the design.

It must perform a real analytical function: clarify what a quotation means for the research question, explain a theme's significance, surface real variation/tension/ambiguity, connect a theme to the declared method, state an interpretive boundary, or tie an implication to an actual finding.

## Step 3 — Interpretive integrity check (reject if any fails)

Research question unchanged; quotations not altered/polished/intensified/softened/mis-shortened; participant meaning not distorted; no new code/category/theme/relationship presented as original analysis; theme names/boundaries/meanings stable; a minority view not rewritten as shared; negative cases/tensions/ambiguity not deleted for a cleaner narrative; reflexivity not describing unrecorded experience; no unperformed procedure (pilot, multiple coding rounds, inter-coder agreement, member checking, triangulation, saturation, theoretical sampling, audit trail, peer debriefing, memoing, translation/back-translation) claimed; cited literature not overstated; implications grounded; conclusion within study scope.

## Step 4 — Quality check

The revision must make the material→interpretation link clearer, explain theme meaning rather than restate a topic, preserve complexity/variation, avoid turning partial evidence into universal claims, stay compatible with the declared method, keep a formal master's-thesis register, and improve transparency rather than merely vary sentence form. No new colloquialism, emotion, or grammar error.

## Step 5 — Methodology compatibility (do not apply one voice to all)

- Reflexive thematic analysis: themes are interpreted patterns of meaning; justified reflexivity allowed; never present themes as objective facts that simply "emerged".
- Codebook / framework analysis: clarify coding structure, category application, cross-case comparison; no reflexive claims inconsistent with the codebook; no invented coding agreement.
- Grounded theory: preserve category development, constant comparison, conceptual relationships; never invent saturation, theoretical sampling, memoing, or core-category integration.
- Phenomenology / IPA: preserve lived-experience depth and idiographic detail before cross-case claims; no invented experiential depth.
- Case study: preserve case boundaries, context, evidence sources, within/cross-case logic; no new case facts or causal claims.
- Narrative inquiry: preserve sequence, turning points, temporality, voice; do not fragment narratives without justification.
- Discourse analysis: preserve attention to language use, positioning, power; do not reduce findings to simple opinions.
- Ethnography: preserve field context, observed practice, researcher position; never invent observations or interactions.
- Document analysis: preserve source, context, textual evidence, limits; no claims about actors' intentions unsupported by the documents.
- Focus groups: preserve interaction, agreement/disagreement, co-construction; do not rewrite group talk as isolated opinion.

If the method is unclear, restrict revision to clarity, evidence connection, and removing generic wording until the method is confirmed.

## Allowed / conditional / prohibited

ALLOWED: evidence-grounded cautious interpretation; theme-meaning clarification within existing boundaries; making real variation/tension visible with accurate quantifiers ("several participants", "in some accounts", "this was not shared uniformly"); clearer theme↔research-question links; literature comparison without distortion; grounded implications; study-specific boundaries; de-templating repetitive structure to expose the analytical pathway.

CONDITIONAL (only if the study genuinely contains it): describing interview-guide changes, code/theme evolution, field notes, memos, member checking, triangulation, peer/audit procedures, saturation/information-power judgments, why a quote was chosen as representative/deviant, researcher–participant relationship, researcher influence. Absent real basis, mark for author confirmation — do not write it as finished prose.

PROHIBITED: inventing quotations/observations/field details/emotions/reactions; editing quotations for fluency/drama; making one quote prove a broad theme; deleting negative cases; inventing codes/categories/themes/relationships/stages; claiming unperformed procedures; adding reflections not part of the real analysis; deliberate language flaws or fake non-native errors; random structural scrambling; rewriting mainly to bypass detection; claiming any technique will certainly change a detector score.

## Special rules

Prevent quote decoration: a key quotation must do real analytical work (show how a participant experiences the phenomenon, reveal variation/tension, support a theme's meaning, illustrate a process/context, or evidence a boundary case). Do not add explanation a quotation cannot bear.

Prevent topic-only themes: a broad label (e.g. "Work Pressure", "AI Use") may be clarified to its actual analytical meaning ONLY if the existing analysis supports it and the refinement does not change meaning, evidence base, boundaries, merge themes, or require new analysis. Otherwise mark for analytical review.

Prevent false reflexivity: reflexivity is allowed only when methodologically appropriate and supported by real records. Never invent researcher surprise/emotion, participant demeanour, "a moment that changed my understanding", coding struggles, relationships, or power/access dynamics not documented. Where the design supports a cautious boundary (e.g. participants may have been guarded about management topics), phrase it as a setting limitation, not an observed fact.

Preserve divergence: do not delete minority views, collapse mixed accounts into a single theme, or replace cautious wording with universal claims; analytically meaningful inconsistency is a finding, not a flaw.

Ground implications and limitations: implications must depend on an actual theme/finding; limitations must name the real participant group, setting, access, data source, case selection, researcher position, language/translation, sensitivity, or interpretive scope — never invented fieldwork problems.

## Mixed-methods

Apply quantitative rules to statistical/hypothesis/measurement passages and these qualitative rules to interview/coding/thematic passages. For integration passages, do not rewrite qualitative findings as mere illustrations of the numbers unless that was the actual design, do not overstate quantitative results via quotes, preserve agreement/complementarity/contradiction accurately, and add no new integrated conclusion unsupported by both the results and the design.

## Participant confidentiality

Revision must preserve anonymisation. Do not add, infer, reconstruct, or expose identifying details about participants, organisations, locations, departments, job titles, or roles that were anonymised or omitted in the original thesis. Do not combine scattered context across paragraphs in a way that would make an anonymised participant identifiable. Participant labels (P1, Manager 3, etc.) stay exactly as in the original; never replace a label with a name or an identifying descriptor. If a revision would increase identifiability, do not make it.

## Core decision standard

A qualitative authorship-restoration revision is legitimate only when the new wording makes the analytical pathway more transparent WITHOUT altering original materials, inventing procedures, suppressing meaningful differences, fabricating reflection, or extending interpretation beyond the study's evidence. When traceability cannot be established, mark the passage for author confirmation or renewed analysis rather than rewriting it as finished content.

Insufficient-material branch: if the thesis does not provide enough quotes, codes, memos, or documented method detail to support a restoration, do NOT add any new interpretive researcher voice. Only the safe, content-neutral operations remain available — de-templating repeated phrasing and varying sentence rhythm using the wording already present, plus accurate scope/limitation statements grounded in the real design. Label such a passage "Author confirmation needed" so the author can supply the missing material; never close the gap by inventing it.
